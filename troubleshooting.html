AC Blowing Warm Air
▼
What You Notice
Air from your vents feels warm or room temperature instead of cool, even when the AC is running.
Most Common Cause
Low refrigerant (Freon) or a dirty, clogged air filter.
What this means: Refrigerant is the substance that absorbs heat from inside your home and releases it outside — it's what actually makes the air cold. When refrigerant is low, your system can't cool properly. A clogged air filter can also restrict airflow so severely that the system struggles to cool the air at all.
Suggested Action
Check and replace your air filter first — this takes minutes and is free if you have a spare filter on hand. If warm air continues after that, the refrigerant level needs to be checked by a licensed technician. Refrigerant issues cannot be repaired without professional equipment and certification.

AC Not Turning On
▼
What You Notice
The system won't start at all when the thermostat calls for cooling — no fan, no airflow, nothing.
Most Common Cause
Tripped circuit breaker, thermostat setting issue, or a blown fuse at the unit.
What this means: Before assuming the worst, check the simple things first. A tripped breaker at your electrical panel is one of the most common reasons an AC won't start — and it takes 30 seconds to fix. It's also very common for the thermostat to be set to "Fan Only" or "Heat" by accident, especially after a season change.
Suggested Action
Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker labeled "AC" or "Air Handler" and reset it if needed. Make sure your thermostat is set to Cool mode with the target temperature set lower than the current room temperature. If the system still won't start after those checks, a technician needs to take a closer look.
Still not turning on? We'll send a technician out to diagnose the problem quickly and get your AC back up and running.

AC Running But Not Cooling the House
▼
What You Notice
The system runs continuously but the house stays warm — the temperature never reaches your thermostat setting.
Most Common Cause
Dirty outdoor condenser coils, refrigerant issue, or a system that's undersized for the home.
What this means: The outdoor unit (condenser) releases the heat pulled from your home. If its coils are clogged with dirt, grass, or debris, heat gets trapped and the system can't cool efficiently. A refrigerant leak has a similar effect. On extremely hot days, a system that wasn't properly sized for your home may also struggle to keep up.
Suggested Action
Walk outside and check the outdoor unit — make sure it's not surrounded by overgrown shrubs or debris, and that the fins aren't caked with dirt. Give it a few inches of clearance on all sides. If it looks clean and clear and the problem continues, a service call is the next step.
House still not cooling? Our technicians will find the root cause and have your system working the way it should.

AC Turning On and Off Frequently
▼
What You Notice
The AC starts up, runs for just a short time, shuts off — then repeats the cycle over and over. The house never fully cools down.
Most Common Cause
Oversized system, dirty air filter causing overheating, or low refrigerant.
What this means: This is called "short cycling," and it's hard on your equipment. An oversized AC cools the space too quickly and shuts off before properly removing humidity — which can leave your home feeling clammy even when the temperature reads correctly. A clogged air filter can also cause the system to overheat and trigger a safety shutoff repeatedly.
Suggested Action
Start by replacing the air filter and making sure all supply vents throughout the house are open and unobstructed. If the short cycling continues, have a technician inspect the system — persistent short cycling adds wear and can lead to premature equipment failure.
Short cycling is hard on your system. The sooner it's diagnosed, the better. Give us a call and we'll get someone out to take a look.

AC Making Loud or Unusual Noises
▼
What You Notice
Banging, rattling, squealing, grinding, or clanking sounds coming from the indoor or outdoor unit.
Most Common Cause
Loose components, a failing fan motor, debris caught in the unit, or worn bearings.
What this means: Unusual sounds are almost always a mechanical warning sign. A banging or clanking sound often points to something loose or broken inside the unit. A squealing noise typically means a worn belt or failing motor bearing. A rattling sound could be debris caught in the fan. None of these get better on their own.
Suggested Action
Turn the system off and call for service. Running a unit that's making loud noises can quickly turn a smaller repair into a much larger one — or cause total equipment failure. Don't wait on this one.
Unusual sounds mean something's wrong. Call us right away — catching it early almost always means a less costly repair.

AC Leaking Water
▼
What You Notice
Water pooling around the indoor air handler, dripping from the unit, or staining on the ceiling or walls near the unit.
Most Common Cause
Clogged condensate drain line.
What this means: As your AC cools the air, it also removes humidity — and that moisture has to go somewhere. It drains through a small condensate drain line that can clog over time with algae, dust, or debris. When it clogs, water backs up and overflows the drain pan. Left untreated, this can cause water damage to walls, ceilings, and flooring.
Suggested Action
Locate the condensate drain line access point (usually a small PVC pipe near the air handler) and try pouring a cup of diluted white vinegar or bleach into it to clear a mild clog. If the leaking continues or is significant, turn off the system and call for service to prevent water damage.
Water damage can happen fast. If the leak isn't clearing up, give us a call and we'll take care of it.

AC Frozen Coil (Ice on the Unit)
▼
What You Notice
Ice or frost forming on the indoor unit or the copper refrigerant lines. Airflow may be weak or the air may feel warm despite the AC running.
Most Common Cause
Restricted airflow from a dirty filter, or low refrigerant causing the coil to drop below freezing.
What this means: Your evaporator coil needs steady airflow to stay at the right temperature. When airflow is blocked (usually by a clogged filter), or refrigerant is too low, the coil gets cold enough to freeze the moisture in the air around it. A frozen coil can't cool your home, and if the system keeps running, it can damage the compressor — the most expensive part of the system.
Suggested Action
Turn the system to Fan Only mode (not Off, not Cool) to allow the coil to thaw — this can take 2–4 hours. Replace the air filter while you wait. Once fully thawed, restart the system in cooling mode. If it freezes again within a day, the refrigerant level needs to be checked by a technician.
Repeated freezing can damage your compressor. If it keeps happening, call us — we'll find the cause and fix it before it becomes a bigger problem.

Weak Airflow from Vents
▼
What You Notice
Very little air coming from the vents even when the system is running — rooms take a long time to heat or cool, or feel stuffy.
Most Common Cause
Dirty or clogged air filter, blocked vents, or a problem with the ductwork or blower fan.
What this means: Poor airflow is one of the most common HVAC complaints and is often caused by something simple — a clogged filter or a vent that was accidentally closed. Over time, poor airflow puts extra strain on your blower motor and can reduce the lifespan of the whole system. If the filter and vents check out, ductwork damage or a failing blower could be the culprit.
Suggested Action
Replace the air filter and check every supply vent in the house — make sure none are closed or blocked by furniture. If airflow is still noticeably weak after those steps, the ductwork or blower fan may need to be inspected by a technician.
Poor airflow affects your whole system. We can inspect the blower, ducts, and filter system and restore proper airflow to your home.

Furnace Not Turning On
▼
What You Notice
No heat at all — the furnace won't start when the thermostat calls for it. No sound, no fan, no warmth.
Most Common Cause
No power to the furnace, thermostat setting issue, tripped breaker, or pilot light is out (older units).
What this means: Most of the time when a furnace won't turn on, it's something simple. The power switch near the furnace (it looks like a standard light switch) may have been accidentally turned off. A tripped circuit breaker is another quick fix. And it's surprisingly common for the thermostat to be set to the wrong mode after a season change.
Suggested Action
Check the furnace power switch (usually on the wall near the unit), check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker, and confirm the thermostat is set to Heat mode with the temperature set above the current room temperature. If your furnace has a standing pilot light, follow the manufacturer's directions to relight it. If none of these steps solve it, the ignition system or another component may need professional attention.
Still no heat? Don't stay cold — call us and we'll get a technician out as quickly as possible.

Heater Blowing Cold Air
▼
What You Notice
The furnace or air handler is running and you can feel air from the vents — but it's cold or lukewarm instead of warm.
Most Common Cause
Overheated furnace (burners shut off by a safety switch), or an ignition failure where the burners aren't lighting.
What this means: When a furnace overheats — often because a dirty filter is blocking airflow — a safety limit switch shuts off the burners to prevent damage, but the blower fan keeps running. This pushes unheated air through the vents. You feel air coming out, but it's cold. An ignition issue (where the burners simply fail to light) has the same result.
Suggested Action
Replace the air filter and make sure all vents in the house are open. Turn the system off for 30 minutes to let it cool, then try again. If the problem continues, the limit switch, ignition system, or heat exchanger may need to be evaluated by a technician.
Cold air from the heater is a clear signal something's off. We'll find the cause and get your heat working the way it should.

Uneven Heating in the House
▼
What You Notice
Some rooms are comfortable while others are noticeably colder, even with the heat running. Often affects rooms farther from the furnace or on upper floors.
Most Common Cause
Closed or blocked vents, duct imbalance, or inadequate duct distribution for the home's layout.
What this means: Uneven heating is common, especially in older homes or homes with long duct runs. Heat rises naturally, so upper floors tend to stay warmer. Rooms at the far end of the duct system may receive less airflow. Closed vents in one room don't redirect heat to another — they actually pressure the system and reduce overall efficiency.
Suggested Action
Make sure every supply vent in the house is open. Check that furniture, rugs, or curtains aren't blocking airflow. If certain rooms are consistently cold regardless, a technician can evaluate your duct system and recommend adjustments, duct balancing, or zoning solutions.
Tired of cold rooms? We can evaluate your duct system and help distribute heat more evenly throughout your home.

Furnace Cycling On and Off Frequently
▼
What You Notice
The furnace turns on, runs for a short time, shuts off, then almost immediately starts up again — repeatedly, without the house warming up properly.
Most Common Cause
Overheating due to restricted airflow (dirty filter), or a failing limit switch that's tripping too early.
What this means: A furnace that short cycles is working against itself — it can't build up enough heat before shutting off, and the constant starting and stopping is stressful on the equipment. The most common trigger is a clogged filter causing the furnace to overheat, which trips the high-limit safety switch and shuts things down. A worn limit switch can also cause this on its own, even if the furnace isn't actually overheating.
Suggested Action
Replace the air filter — this alone resolves the issue in many cases. Make sure all vents are open and unblocked. If the cycling continues after those steps, have a technician inspect the limit switch and check for any other underlying cause.
Frequent cycling shortens equipment life. Let us diagnose it before it turns into a bigger repair down the road.

Thermostat Not Responding
▼
What You Notice
Adjusting the thermostat has no effect on the system. The display may be blank, dim, or behaving erratically. The heat or AC doesn't respond to changes.
Most Common Cause
Dead batteries, a tripped circuit breaker, loose wiring behind the unit, or a faulty thermostat.
What this means: The thermostat is the brain of your HVAC system — when it stops communicating, nothing works right. The good news is that it's often something simple. Dead batteries in a battery-powered thermostat will cause the display to go blank and the system to stop responding entirely. A setting that was accidentally changed (like switching from Heat to Cool) can have the same effect.
Suggested Action
Replace the thermostat batteries if it uses them (check the back of the unit). Make sure it's set to the correct mode — Heat or Cool — and that the fan is set to Auto, not On. If the display is blank and fresh batteries don't help, check the circuit breaker. If it still doesn't respond, a technician can test the wiring and replace the unit if needed.
Thermostat issues are usually quick fixes. We can diagnose and replace it quickly to restore full control over your system.
